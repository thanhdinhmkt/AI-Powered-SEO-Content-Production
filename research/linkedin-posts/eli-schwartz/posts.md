## Post 2 — The AI Search Panic Might Be the Best Thing to Ever Happen to SEO

The AI search panic might be the best thing to ever happen to SEO.

Not because AEO is some magic new discipline — it's not new. (See my many posts on this topic.)

But because, for the first time in years, the C-suite is actually asking about organic.

Here's the reality most SEO teams know too well:
- Performance marketing got the budget
- Paid got the headcount
- SEO got a Notion doc and a prayer

Organic has always been the channel that quietly drove outsized returns while being chronically underfunded. It sat low on the org chart, dependent on engineering prioritization, and perpetually one reorg away from being folded into content or comms.

Now, suddenly, because LLMs exist and people are worried about traffic, everyone wants to talk about it.

Executives are asking how they show up in AI answers.
Boards want to understand search visibility.
Budget conversations that used to stall are moving.

It's fine with me that some of the AEO discourse is overblown because the noise gets SEO a seat at the table, which will translate into real headcount and a budget that reflects the channel's actual value.

## Post 3 — AEO Is Not SEO 2.0

AEO is not SEO 2.0.

And everyone treating it like an upgrade has already missed the point.

SEO was always about a funnel that began with search traffic, so technical precision and link volume were key parts of the playbook. You optimized pages, chased rankings, and measured everything in clicks.

LLMs don't have rankings, don't have a page one, and the click stopped being the KPI a while ago. Most people still haven't adjusted their thinking to match that reality.

The part everyone skips over is that you can't manufacture visibility anymore. A scrappy site with the right backlinks could outrank a household name in SEO.

That arbitrage is completely dead now.

LLMs don't respond to domain authority; they respond to whether people actually know who you are, whether your brand gets referenced in the real world.

Citations are not backlinks. You don't acquire your way into an LLM answer by building links or publishing more content.

You get there by being a brand that the world already talked about before you started optimizing anything.

Ranking was something you could game with enough effort and the right tactics. Real trust doesn't work that way.

## Post 4 — AI Writes About AI Search Using 2-Year-Old Marketing Copy

You can tell immediately when AI wrote something about AI search.

It lists Perplexity as a "Google alternative."

No mention of Claude. Hardly a mention of Gemini or Meta.

Here's why: Perplexity spent 2023 loudly positioning itself as a Google killer. That framing got picked up everywhere, baked into articles, tweets, and LinkedIn posts, so when LLMs trained on that data, they absorbed the narrative too.

Now, when you ask an AI to write about AI search, it regurgitates the marketing copy from two years ago. LLMs become a self-referencing cycle.

...But this is a great callout on how LLMs actually do or don't drive conversion. Yes, Perplexity shows up in all that AI content, yet it's not causing people to actually believe it, if you trust the Similarweb data on its usage.

Perplexity won mindshare from the LLMs but not the humans who do the searches.

## Post 5 — Optimizing for LLM Visibility Has a Soft Underbelly

Intentional optimization efforts for LLM visibility have a soft underbelly that many miss.

LLMs don't just read content — they're trained on everything people say about you: the reviews, the forums, the Reddit threads where someone explains exactly why your support ruined their week.

Trying to fake this with gamed citations misses the point.

If you've prompted your way into a five-star recommendation but deliver a three-star experience, you haven't solved anything.

You've added yet another step to the disappointment funnel.

The customer hears about you from an AI, tries you, finds the reality doesn't match, and goes back online to say exactly that.

That is a vicious cycle.

Those new words get scraped → They get trained on → The system corrects itself.

The feedback loop isn't slow the way Google's was because models are getting better at weighting recent, specific, experiential content over polished marketing copy.

Optimize for the prompt, and you're only optimizing for the first impression.

Focus on marketing, product AND experience.
